Chapter 6 Integration

Indefinite integration

  • Integration In calculus, the inverse of differentiation is called integration.

  • Integral In general, if \(F‚Ä≤(x) = f(x)\), then \(F(x)\) is said to be the integral (sometimes called the anti-derivative or primitive) of \(f(x)\) and is written \[F(x)=\int f(x)dx.\]

  • Constant of integration In general, if \(F(x)\) is any function that differentiates to \(f(x)\), then so does \(F(x) + c\). The arbitrary constant, \(c\), is called the constant of integration.

Definite integration

  • Definite integral In general, the definite integral \[\int_{a}^b f(x)dx\] denotes the area under the graph of \(f(x)\) between \(x = a\) and \(x = b\). The numbers \(a\) and \(b\) are called the limits of integration.

  • Consumer‚Äôs surplus The excess cost that a person would have been prepared to pay for goods over and above what is actually paid.

  • Producer‚Äôs surplus The excess revenue that a producer has actually received over and above the lower revenue that it was prepared to accept for the supply of its goods.

  • Net investment Rate of change of capital stock over time: \[I = \frac{dK}{dt}.\]
